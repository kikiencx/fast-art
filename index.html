AI Art and Sustainability in the UK

As generative AI art tools continue to gain popularity, particularly in creative industries and digital design, concerns about their environmental sustainability have come to the forefront. Although AI-generated images may seem intangible, each interaction and output consumes a notable amount of energy and water and raises questions about the ecological footprint of AI creativity, especially in the UK. As the UK embraces AI in creative sectors, the sustainability of this technology has become a pressing issue.

Creating art with AI may seem amazing to many users, but each image involves running large-scale machine learning models in data centres that requires a lot of computing power. According to The Verge (2025), generating a single AI image consumes approximately 0.0029 kilowatts–hours (kWh) which is about the same energy needed to charge a smartphone. While this may appear trivial, the scale of global usage changes the idea dramatically. Platforms processing thousands or even millions of image prompts daily increase this demand exponentially.

Tools such as ChatGPT require a lot more electricity than traditional digital services. A study cited in Nature (2025) reports that a single interaction with a generative AI tool consumes 10 times more electricity than a basic Google search.

In the UK, the growing demand is reflected in the rise of energy-intensive data centres, particularly in London and the South East. The Greater London Authority has acknowledged that data centre growth is accelerating due to AI workloads, and efforts are underway to understand how this will affect the city’s carbon emissions and climate budgeting strategies.

The trend mirrors what's happening globally. Power requirements for data centres in North America rose from 2,688 megawatts in 2022 to 5,341 megawatts in 2023, with generative AI being a major factor. This doubling of energy demand in just one year reflects the intensity of AI development and deployment, and the UK is not far behind.

Electricity is only one part of the story. Cooling systems in data centres often rely on large volumes of freshwater, a lesser-known but critical sustainability concern. As these centres work around the clock to deliver AI-generated content they also require water to prevent overheating.

AI data centres can use between 1.8 and 12 litres of water per kWh of energy consumed, depending on the cooling technology and location. This means that current AI workloads could require roughly 18 billion to 120 billion litres of water each year. In parts of southern England, the expansion of AI infrastructure could exacerbate resource scarcity. The government has considered building new reservoirs, such as the proposed Abingdon reservoir, which was designed to address the growing water demand in regions such as London to secure water supply against drought and population growth, to help meet future demand from both the population and the tech sector.

The environmental toll of AI-generated art is not just in the moment of image creation: it is produced along the entire AI supply chain. According to the Art of Procurement blog, the increasing demand for more processing power and high performance data storage is reshaping global supply chains toward increased production. This means more mining of raw materials, more manufacturing, and more international shipping, all of which generate higher carbon emissions.

Even the training phase of AI models can have a lot of energy demands. Large models, including those behind generative art platforms, may require millions of hours of computing power to train them, which results in a lot of carbon emissions. Although training is a one time process, updates and fine tuning add to the carbon footprint over time.

In response to these concerns, the UK government has announced support for AI businesses, funding initiatives that encourage low impact computing, green energy integration and improved data centre efficiency.

Some AI companies are also taking steps to reduce their carbon footprint by adopting liquid cooling systems which is a method used to cool electronic components by circulating a liquid coolant, usually water, through a loop that includes the components needing cooling or to invest in renewable energy. But critics say green initiatives are not keeping up with the increasing growth of generative AI. Without stronger regulation or public awareness, sustainability risks being overshadowed by the excitement around AI’s creative potential.

It has become easier to generate hundreds of images at the click of a button and although the environmental cost per image may be small the volume of output is growing. A report from the website ‘AIPRM’ noted that users of popular tools produce over 10,000 images a month with limited use or engagement beyond creation.

So are we using AI art efficiently, or flooding digital spaces with disposable content? Sustainable creativity may require us to shift from quantity to quality, encouraging slower engagement with AI tools and a clearer understanding of their hidden costs.

Policy action, public awareness and innovation are all needed to ensure that the future of AI-generated art is not only imaginative, but also environmentally responsible. Without this balance, the artistic freedom offered by AI may come at the expense of the ecosystems and resources we depend on.
